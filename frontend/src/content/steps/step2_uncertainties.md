## Key Uncertainties — Air Quality

### Exposure measurement error

Neither monitors nor satellite products measure personal exposure directly. Ambient outdoor concentrations are a proxy. People spend ~90% of time indoors where infiltration factors, indoor sources, and activity patterns modify true exposure (Breen et al., 2014).

### Satellite model uncertainty

Satellite-derived PM2.5 estimates combine multiple data sources through statistical fusion. Each step introduces uncertainty: satellite retrieval of aerosol optical depth, chemical transport model simulations, and ground calibration regression. Estimated uncertainty is typically ±20–35% at the grid cell level.

### Spatial averaging

Using zonal mean concentrations (averaging over an admin polygon) suppresses within-zone variation. In areas with both industrial hotspots and cleaner residential zones, the mean may not represent typical population exposure.

### Counterfactual sensitivity

The choice of control scenario is often the largest driver of uncertainty in HIA results. Changing the benchmark from the WHO guideline (5 μg/m³) to WHO IT-4 (10 μg/m³) can halve the estimated health burden. Always conduct sensitivity analyses across multiple counterfactuals.

### Temporal representativeness

A single year's concentration may reflect unusual meteorological conditions (e.g., wildfire seasons, El Niño). Multi-year averages reduce this volatility but delay the analysis.
