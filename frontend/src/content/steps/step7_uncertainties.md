## Key Uncertainties — Valuation

### VSL estimate range

The EPA's central VSL of $11.8M is drawn from a distribution. The underlying meta-analysis of 26 studies yields estimates ranging from roughly $1M to $21M. The central value is a policy choice, not a precise measurement.

### Income elasticity uncertainty

The income elasticity of VSL is one of the most debated parameters in environmental economics. Empirical estimates range from 0.5 to 1.5. A meta-analysis by Viscusi & Masterman (2017) found a central estimate near 1.0, but with considerable heterogeneity across studies.

For a country with GNI per capita at 10% of the U.S. level:
- ε = 0.8 → transferred VSL ≈ 16% of U.S. VSL
- ε = 1.0 → transferred VSL ≈ 10% of U.S. VSL
- ε = 1.2 → transferred VSL ≈ 6% of U.S. VSL

### PPP vs. market exchange rates

The tool uses **PPP (purchasing power parity)** GNI per capita, which accounts for cost-of-living differences. Market exchange rates would give lower GNI ratios for developing countries, further reducing transferred VSL values.

### Valuing morbidity

Morbidity valuation is even more uncertain. Cost-of-illness methods capture only direct costs and lost earnings. Willingness-to-pay for avoided illness (pain, suffering, reduced quality of life) is typically 2–5× higher than COI estimates.

### Discounting and time horizon

Should future health benefits be discounted? At a 3% discount rate, a death averted 20 years from now is worth only 55% of one averted today. The choice of discount rate can dominate the valuation result for long-term scenarios.

### Ethical considerations

Any monetary valuation of health outcomes raises ethical questions. Present valuations transparently, acknowledge limitations, and consider presenting results as both health-only and monetized.
