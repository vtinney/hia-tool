## Key Uncertainties — Study Area

### Geographic boundary mismatch

Administrative boundaries rarely align perfectly with air quality monitoring grids or satellite-derived concentration surfaces. Polygon boundaries used for zonal statistics may include heterogeneous exposure levels, particularly for large or irregularly shaped units.

### Pollutant interaction

Selecting a single pollutant treats it as the sole driver of health effects. In reality, ambient pollutants are correlated — areas with high PM2.5 often have elevated NO₂ and ozone. Multi-pollutant HIA methods exist but are not yet standard practice (Dominici et al., 2010).

### Temporal alignment

If your concentration data, population, and incidence rates are from different years, you are assuming that the spatial patterns remained stable over that period. This is reasonable for 1–3 year gaps but becomes problematic for longer spans, especially in rapidly developing regions.

### Ecological fallacy

An area-level HIA assigns average exposure to an entire population, ignoring within-area variation in both exposure and susceptibility. This is an inherent limitation of ecological study designs.
