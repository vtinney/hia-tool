## Key Uncertainties — Population

### Census accuracy and timeliness

Census data is typically collected every 10 years. Between censuses, populations are estimated using intercensal models that assume smooth trends. Rapid urbanization, migration, or displacement events can make intercensal estimates inaccurate.

### Modeled population grids

WorldPop and GPWv4 disaggregate census counts to grid cells using statistical models. The grid cell population is an estimate, not a measurement. Uncertainty is highest in rural areas with sparse settlement data and in informal urban settlements.

### Age distribution uncertainty

National age pyramids may not reflect subnational variation. Urban populations tend to be younger than rural populations due to labor migration. Using a national age distribution for a city-level HIA may bias age-specific health burden estimates.

### Population-at-risk vs. total population

Not everyone in the study area is equally exposed. Indoor workers, people with air conditioning, and those who relocated during the study period may have different effective exposures. HIAs typically assume the entire resident population is at risk.

### Double-counting across age-restricted CRFs

If you apply both an all-ages CRF and an age-restricted CRF to the same endpoint, you may double-count a subpopulation. Ensure CRF age ranges do not overlap for the same health outcome.
