## Best Practices — Valuation

1. **Report health outcomes first, valuation second.** The primary output of an HIA is attributable cases and deaths. Monetary valuation adds a policy-relevant dimension but should not replace the health estimates.

2. **Present a range of valuations.** Use at least two elasticity values (e.g., 0.8 and 1.0) and report the corresponding valuation range. This communicates the sensitivity to this parameter.

3. **Use PPP-adjusted GNI for benefit transfer.** PPP rates better reflect the purchasing power of populations in different countries than market exchange rates (OECD, 2012).

4. **State your VSL source and year.** Report whether you use the EPA central estimate, an OECD value, or a country-specific study. Adjust for inflation to a consistent dollar year using the GDP deflator.

5. **Do not use income-adjusted VSL without disclosure.** If you apply OECD benefit transfer to a low-income country, explicitly state the resulting VSL and the elasticity used. Readers should understand that the valuation reflects income differences, not differences in the value of lives.

6. **Consider presenting results per capita or as a share of GDP.** This normalizes the economic burden and facilitates comparison across countries of different sizes and income levels.

7. **For morbidity endpoints, use willingness-to-pay where available.** Cost-of-illness undervalues health impacts. If WTP estimates exist for your endpoints (e.g., from BenMAP-CE defaults), prefer them over COI.

8. **Acknowledge what valuation does not capture.** Pain, suffering, ecosystem damage, lost recreation, and intergenerational equity are real costs that standard VSL approaches do not fully monetize.
